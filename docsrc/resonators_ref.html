Resonators

Most EPR experiments are performed with samples placed in microwave resonators. These resonators serve a dual purpose:

  • they minimize the microwave electric field E1 at the sample, thereby minimizing the impact of electric-dipole transitions and heating, and
  • they maximize the microwave magnetic field B1 at the sample, thereby maximizing sensitivity for the weak magnetic-dipole transitions observed in EPR.

There are many types of microwave resonators that differ in their construction, but all of them are characterized by a set of basic parameters:

  • The resonator frequency, , is the frequency at which the resonators admits most power.
  • The intrinsic (unloaded) Q (quality) factor, , characterizes the microwave power losses in the resonator - the lower the losses the higher the Q. The unloaded Q factors for resonators used in CW EPR can be up to 10,000 or more. For pulse EPR, lower unloaded Q values of several hundred are used.
  • The coupling coefficient between the resonator and the transmission line, . This positive number characterizes how well the impedance of the resonator is matched to the impedance of the transmission line it is connected to (waveguide, coaxial cable, etc.). A value of 1 corresponds to perfect match, the resonator is said to be critically coupled, and at the resonator frequency 100% of the incident microwave power is admitted into the resonator. If is less than 1, the resonator is undercoupled, and if it is larger than 1, the resonator is overcoupled. In either case, microwave power is partially reflected even at the resonator frequency, either in-phase (when undercoupled) or in anti-phase (when overcoupled).

Resonators admit power only around their resonance frequency, in a frequency band determined by the unloaded Q factor and the coupling coefficient.
